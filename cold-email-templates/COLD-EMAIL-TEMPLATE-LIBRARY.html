Part I: Introduction

How to Use This Library

Quick Start (5 Minutes)

  1. Identify Your Situation

    • What industry are you in? (SaaS, Agency, B2B Services)
    • What's your goal? (Book meetings, get demos, build partnerships)
    • Find the matching sequence in the Table of Contents

  2. Read the Full Sequence

    • Each sequence has 5 emails designed to work together
    • Don't cherry-pick individual emails - use the complete sequence
    • Review timing guidelines (Day 0, Day 3, Day 7, etc.)

  3. Personalize Each Email

    • Spend 5 minutes researching each prospect (see Personalization Framework)
    • Replace [brackets] with specific information
    • Customize opening lines and specific observations

  4. Track & Optimize

    • Monitor open rates, response rates, meeting bookings
    • A/B test subject lines and opening paragraphs
    • Double down on what works for your audience

Template Selection Guide

Choose By Industry:

SaaS Companies (Software products) → Use SaaS sequences

  • Meeting Booking: Book discovery calls with potential customers
  • Demo Request: Get prospects to see your product
  • Partnership: Build integrations or co-marketing with other SaaS

Agencies (Marketing, design, development services) → Use Agency sequences

  • Meeting Booking: Book initial consultations with clients
  • Portfolio Review: Get prospects to review your work
  • Partnership: Build referral networks or white-label relationships

B2B Services (Consulting, legal, accounting, HR, etc.) → Use B2B Services sequences

  • Meeting Booking: Book discovery calls with service buyers
  • Discovery Call: Offer free assessments or consultations
  • Partnership: Build referral partnerships with complementary services

Choose By Goal:

Goal: Book Meetings

  • Use "Meeting Booking" sequences
  • Focus: Getting 15-30 minute calls on calendar
  • Best for: Initial prospecting, cold outreach

Goal: Get Product Demos

  • Use "Demo Request" or "Portfolio Review" sequences
  • Focus: Showing your product or work examples
  • Best for: Sales-driven products, visual work

Goal: Build Partnerships

  • Use "Partnership" sequences
  • Focus: Strategic relationships, referrals, integrations
  • Best for: B2B partnerships, ecosystem plays

Goal: Re-engage Old Leads

  • Use Bonus Templates: Re-engagement, Event, Content Share
  • Focus: Warming up cold prospects
  • Best for: Leads that went cold 6+ months ago

The 5-Minute Personalization Method {#personalization-method}

Why personalization matters:

  • Generic emails get <1% response rate
  • Personalized emails get 8-15% response rate
  • 5 minutes of research = 10x better results

The 5-Minute Research Breakdown:

Minute 1: LinkedIn Profile

  • Current role & tenure
  • Recent posts/activity
  • Shared connections
  • Previous companies

Minute 2: Company Website

  • What they do
  • Recent blog posts/news
  • Customer logos
  • Job openings

Minute 3: Trigger Events

  • Funding (Crunchbase)
  • Product launches
  • Executive hires
  • Expansion news

Minute 4: Competitive Context

  • Who their competitors are
  • Tools they likely use
  • Industry challenges
  • Market trends

Minute 5: Social Quick Check

  • Twitter/X activity
  • Company social media
  • Reddit/community presence
  • Recent content

Personalization Insertion Points:

  1. Subject Line: Reference specific observation
  2. Opening Line: Most critical - hooks or loses them in 5 seconds
  3. Problem Statement: Connect to their specific situation
  4. Social Proof: Similar company/industry
  5. Call to Action: Relevant to their needs

Rule: Never send an email without personalizing at least the opening line.

SaaS - Meeting Booking Sequence

Industry: SaaS Use Case: Meeting Booking / Discovery Call Sequence Length: 5 emails over 30 days Best For: Booking meetings with potential customers to discuss their challenges

Email 1: Initial Outreach (Day 0)

Subject: Quick question about [Company Name]'s [specific process]

Body:

Hi [First Name],

I noticed [Company Name] is [specific observation from their website/LinkedIn - e.g., "expanding into the European market" or "recently raised Series B funding"].

Most [their role/industry] teams I work with struggle with [specific pain point related to your solution]. For example, one VP of Sales I spoke with was spending 15+ hours weekly on [manual task your product automates].

We built [Your Product] specifically to help [their company type] teams [achieve specific outcome] without [common drawback].

Worth a 15-minute conversation?

[Your Name]

Personalization Notes:

  • Replace [specific observation] with something from their recent LinkedIn posts, company blog, or press releases
  • Use [their role/industry] that matches their actual position
  • Reference [specific pain point] that's relevant to their company stage/size

Email 2: Value-Add Follow-Up (Day 3-4)

Subject: Re: Quick question about [Company Name]'s [specific process]

Body:

Hi [First Name],

I know inboxes get busy. Wanted to share something that might be useful regardless of whether we talk.

We recently published a guide on [relevant topic to their pain point] - specifically for [their industry/role]. It covers:

• [Specific tactic #1] • [Specific tactic #2] • [Specific tactic #3]

Here's the link: [actual resource link]

Also happy to jump on a quick call if you're exploring solutions for [their pain point] - we've helped [similar company type] companies like [Name Drop 1] and [Name Drop 2] reduce [metric] by [percentage/number].

Best time for a 15-min chat next week?

[Your Name]

Personalization Notes:

  • Provide actual value (guide, template, checklist) - not gated content
  • Name drop companies similar in size/industry to theirs
  • Use specific metrics (not vague "improve efficiency")

Email 3: Social Proof / Case Study (Day 7)

Subject: How [Similar Company] solved [specific problem]

Body:

Hi [First Name],

Following up one more time because I recently helped [Similar Company Name] tackle the exact challenge I mentioned.

Their situation: They were [specific problem context] which caused [negative outcome - time lost, revenue impact, team frustration].

What we did: Implemented [Your Product]'s [specific feature] to [specific action].

Result:

  • [Metric improvement #1 with specific number/percentage]
  • [Metric improvement #2 with specific number/percentage]
  • [Qualitative benefit - e.g., "Their SDR team cut prospecting time from 3 hours to 45 minutes daily"]

Full case study here: [link]

Since [Company Name] is also [relevant similarity to case study company], I thought this might resonate.

15 minutes to explore if this could work for your team?

[Your Name]

Personalization Notes:

  • Use a case study from their specific industry or company stage
  • Focus on outcomes they'd care about (revenue, time saved, team efficiency)
  • Draw explicit parallel between case study company and their situation

Email 4: Break-Up Email (Day 10-12)

Subject: Should I close your file?

Body:

Hi [First Name],

I've reached out a few times about [specific value proposition] but haven't heard back - which usually means:

  1. Not a priority right now
  2. Wrong timing
  3. You're handling [pain point] a different way
  4. My emails aren't relevant

If it's #1 or #2, no problem at all - should I follow up in [3/6] months instead?

If it's #3 or #4, I'll close your file and won't bother you again.

Either way, appreciate you reading this far.

[Your Name]

P.S. - If there is interest but you're just swamped, this Calendly link makes it easy to grab time without the back-and-forth: [calendly link]

Personalization Notes:

  • Be direct and respectful of their time
  • Give them an easy out or an easy in
  • The P.S. often gets the most responses in break-up emails

Subject: [Industry] trend I thought you'd find interesting

Body:

Hi [First Name],

I know we couldn't connect earlier, but wanted to flag something I'm seeing across [their industry] companies right now.

We're noticing that [specific trend observation] - for example:

  • [Specific data point or industry shift]
  • [How it's impacting companies like theirs]
  • [Strategic implication]

[Optional: Soft pitch paragraph] We're helping [their industry] teams adapt to this by [brief solution description]. Happy to share what's working if you're thinking about this challenge.

Hope this is useful either way.

[Your Name]

Personalization Notes:

  • Lead with genuine insight or value, not a pitch
  • Reference recent industry news, regulatory changes, or market shifts
  • Make the pitch optional/soft - position yourself as a resource, not a salesperson

When to Pause or Stop Sequence

Pause if they:

  • Open email multiple times but don't respond (they're interested but busy)
  • Reply asking to "circle back in [timeframe]" (set calendar reminder)
  • Engage with your content on LinkedIn/Twitter (warm them up via social first)

Stop if they:

  • Reply with "not interested" (respect it)
  • Mark as spam (unsubscribe them immediately)
  • Have job title/role that's clearly wrong fit (research failure)

Expected Results

Industry Benchmarks for SaaS Cold Email:

  • Response Rate: 5-10% (good), 10-15% (excellent)
  • Meeting Booking Rate: 1-3% of total emails sent
  • Positive Response Rate: 3-7%

To improve results:

  • Personalize first line of Email 1 for every prospect
  • Send fewer, better-targeted emails vs. high volume
  • Test different case studies in Email 3
  • Track which email in sequence gets most replies (usually Email 1 or Email 4)

Demo Best Practices

For Live Demos:

  1. Start with their use case - "Based on our brief chat, sounds like you need to [X]"
  2. Show outcome first, features second - Demo backwards from result
  3. Keep it to 15 minutes max - Respect their time
  4. End with clear next step - "Does this solve [pain]? If yes, I can get you a trial today."

For Recorded Demos:

  1. Keep under 5 minutes - Attention span is limited
  2. Add timestamps - Let them skip to relevant parts
  3. Show real data - Not "example" or "test" accounts
  4. Include voiceover - Don't make them read text

A/B Testing Variations

Subject Line Tests:

  • Email 1 Options:

    • "[Company Name] demo - [Specific Feature]"
    • "15-minute demo for [their industry] teams"
    • "Can I show you how [Similar Company] uses this?"

  • Email 3 Options:

    • "[Customer Name]'s results with [Your Product]"
    • "4-minute case study: [specific outcome]"
    • "[Their industry] team cuts [metric] by [percentage]"

Expected Results

Demo Request Benchmarks:

  • Demo Request Rate: 2-5% of emails sent
  • Demo Show-Up Rate: 60-70% of booked demos
  • Demo-to-Trial Conversion: 40-60%
  • Trial-to-Paid Conversion: 15-25%

Improve demo conversion by:

  • Qualifying harder before booking (ask screening questions)
  • Sending calendar reminder 1 day before + 1 hour before
  • Starting demo with "What's your biggest challenge with [process]?" not "Let me show you features"
  • Following up within 2 hours after demo with trial access + next steps

When to Offer Free Trial vs. Demo

Offer Demo First If:

  • Product is complex (>30 minutes to learn)
  • Price point >$500/month (requires explanation)
  • They need to see customization options
  • Integration setup is non-trivial

Offer Free Trial First If:

  • Product is self-explanatory (clean UX)
  • Price point <$100/month (low-risk)
  • They have hands-on buying style (some industries prefer this)
  • You have strong onboarding automation

Hybrid Approach: Offer both in Email 1: "Want a 15-min demo, or prefer to try it yourself with a free trial?"

Partnership Email Best Practices

Research Before Outreach:

  1. Check their existing partnerships - Look at integrations page, partner directory, press releases
  2. Identify decision maker - Partnership leads, BD team, or sometimes CEO at smaller companies
  3. Find warm intro if possible - Investors, advisors, mutual customers
  4. Understand their partnership model - Do they have a formal program? Revenue share? Technical requirements?

Timing Considerations:

  • Best months: January-March, September-October (budget planning periods)
  • Avoid: November-December (holiday slowdown), July-August (vacation season)
  • Quarterly planning cycles: Many companies plan partnerships quarterly

Types of SaaS Partnerships

1. Integration Partnerships

What: Technical integration between products Best for: Complementary products with shared customers Time investment: High (dev resources required) Value: Sticky, high-value for customers

Email Focus: Technical feasibility, API documentation, customer demand

2. Co-Marketing Partnerships

What: Joint content, webinars, events, campaigns Best for: Similar audiences, different solutions Time investment: Medium (marketing resources) Value: Lead generation, brand awareness

Email Focus: Audience overlap, content ideas, success metrics

3. Referral / Affiliate Partnerships

What: Revenue share for sending customers to each other Best for: Complementary products, no integration needed Time investment: Low (mostly agreement/tracking setup) Value: New customer acquisition channel

Email Focus: Customer overlap data, commission structure, referral volume estimates

4. Reseller Partnerships

What: One company sells the other's product Best for: Established sales channel + new product Time investment: Medium (sales enablement, support) Value: Revenue expansion, market access

Email Focus: Sales team size, target markets, commission/margin expectations

  2. Slide 2: Our customer base (size, industries, roles)
  3. Slide 3: Customer overlap evidence

Keep it visual and data-driven. Avoid heavy text.

Expected Results

Partnership Email Benchmarks:

  • Response Rate: 10-20% (higher than sales emails because less volume)
  • Meeting Booking Rate: 5-10%
  • Partnership Close Rate: 20-40% of meetings → signed agreement
  • Time to Close: 1-3 months on average

Improve partnership outreach by:

  • Getting warm intros from investors, advisors, or mutual customers (5x response rate vs. cold)
  • Targeting companies at similar stage (early-stage to early-stage, etc.)
  • Leading with customer data, not product features
  • Following up persistently but respectfully (partnerships take longer than sales deals)

When to Pursue Partnerships

Good Fit If:

  • Clear customer overlap (>20% of your customers use their product or vice versa)
  • Complementary solutions (not competitive)
  • Similar company stage and values
  • Both have resources to invest in partnership

Bad Fit If:

  • No customer overlap (partnership won't get used)
  • Competitive products (conflict of interest)
  • Mismatched company stage (enterprise vs. startup = different priorities)
  • One-sided benefit (partnerships need mutual value)

Post-Meeting: Partnership Agreement Checklist

If you get to a partnership conversation, clarify these points:

  • Goals: What success looks like for both sides
  • Responsibilities: Who builds/does what
  • Timeline: Key milestones and deadlines
  • Resources: Dev time, marketing budget, sales team involvement
  • Metrics: How you'll measure success
  • Revenue model: Commission, rev share, or non-monetary
  • Term length: 6 months, 1 year, ongoing?
  • Exit clause: How either party can end partnership

Agency Partnership Best Practices

Types of Agency Partnerships

1. Referral Network

Structure: Send overflow or out-of-scope work to each other Best for: Agencies with complementary services Commission: 10-20% of project value or reciprocal value Example: Branding agency refers web development work

2. White-Label Partnership

Structure: One agency delivers work under another's brand Best for: Agencies wanting to offer services without hiring specialists Payment: Wholesale pricing (typically 50-70% of retail) Example: Marketing agency white-labels video production

3. Joint Venture

Structure: Co-pitch and co-deliver large projects Best for: Complementary agencies pursuing bigger opportunities Split: Based on scope of work (usually 50/50 or 60/40) Example: Design agency + development agency co-pitch website redesign

4. Affiliate/Reseller

Structure: One agency sells the other's productized service Best for: Agencies with productized offerings (audits, templates, courses) Commission: 20-30% of sale price Example: SEO agency resells content agency's blog writing service

Partnership Agreement Essentials

Must-Have Terms:

  • Services exchanged - Specific scope of what each party provides
  • Quality standards - How work is reviewed and approved
  • Pricing/commission structure - Clear financial terms
  • Payment terms - Net 30, upfront, milestone-based, etc.
  • Client communication - Who interfaces with end client
  • Liability - Who's responsible if something goes wrong
  • Exclusivity - Can you partner with their competitors?
  • Term and termination - How long partnership lasts, exit terms
  • Confidentiality - Client data, pricing, methods protection
  • Dispute resolution - What happens if there's a conflict

Start simple: Use a 2-page partnership agreement for first few projects, formalize if it works.

Qualifying Questions for Discovery Calls

  1. "What prompted you to take this call?"

    • Identifies urgency and motivation

  2. "What's your current approach to [problem area]?"

  3. "What's the cost of not solving this?"

    • Quantifies pain (helps with pricing later)

  4. "What's your timeline for making a decision?"

    • Qualifies urgency and budget availability

  5. "Who else needs to be involved in this decision?"

    • Identifies if you're talking to decision-maker or influencer

  6. "What's your budget range for this?"

    • Qualifies financial fit (ask tactfully but directly)

Pricing Discussion Framework

For service-based businesses, pricing comes up early. Be prepared:

Option 1: Range-Based

"Most [their company type] companies at your stage invest between $[X]-$[Y] depending on [scope factors]. Does that range work for your planning?"

Option 2: Investment Framing

"This typically represents a $[X] investment with expected return of $[Y] through [specific outcomes]. Does that ROI make sense for [Company Name]?"

Option 3: Tiered Options

"We have three approaches: [Basic] at $[X], [Standard] at $[Y], and [Premium] at $[Z]. Most clients in your situation start with [Standard]."

Never say "it depends" without giving context. Vague pricing kills deals.

  • Budget is dramatically below market rate (will be difficult client)
  • Decision-maker keeps changing (internal dysfunction)
  • They want results guarantees unrealistic for your service
  • They've churned through multiple service providers recently (problem client pattern)
  • They want to pay only on contingency (risk mismatch for most services)

Your expertise is valuable - qualify hard and walk away from bad fits.

  • What happens to in-flight referrals after termination

7. Confidentiality

  • Protection of client information
  • Protection of each other's business practices
  • Non-solicitation of each other's clients (if applicable)

8. Dispute Resolution

  • Mediation/arbitration approach
  • Governing law

Partnership Compensation Models

Option 1: No-Fee Reciprocal

Structure: Free referrals both ways Best for: Similar-sized firms with balanced referral volume Pros: Simple, relationship-focused Cons: Can become unbalanced over time

Option 2: Commission-Based

Structure: X% of fees for referred clients (first year typical) Best for: Unequal referral volume or client value Pros: Fair economic split Cons: Tracking and payment administration

Option 3: Retainer-Based

Structure: Monthly/quarterly retainer for referral priority Best for: Specialized partners you want to "lock in" Pros: Predictable income for specialist Cons: Requires minimum volume to justify

Option 4: Rev-Share for Joint Delivery

Structure: Split project fees based on work contribution Best for: Complex projects requiring both parties Pros: Aligned incentives Cons: More complex to structure and track

Most common: Start with no-fee reciprocal, add commission if volume becomes unbalanced.

Vetting Potential Partners

Due Diligence Checklist:

  • Reputation check - Ask mutual connections, check online reviews
  • Client references - Talk to 2-3 of their current/past clients
  • Work samples - Review their deliverables and quality
  • Communication style - Do they respond promptly and professionally?
  • Values alignment - Do they treat clients the way you do?
  • Financial stability - Are they established and sustainable?
  • Capacity - Can they handle referrals without being overloaded?
  • Insurance/credentials - Do they have appropriate coverage and certifications?

Red flags:

  • Poor reputation in the market
  • Aggressive sales tactics (will reflect on you)
  • Poor communication during partnership discussions
  • Can't provide client references
  • Significantly undercut market rates (quality concern)
  • High team turnover or frequent business model changes

  • Trial → Formal Agreement: 50-70%

Making First Referrals (Proving Value)

Once partnership is agreed:

First Referral Process:

1. Pre-qualify the client

  • Confirm they need partner's service
  • Brief them on your partner's expertise
  • Get client permission to make intro

2. Warm introduction

  • Email intro cc'ing both parties
  • Context on client situation
  • Why you're recommending this partner
  • Clear next step (partner will reach out)

3. Follow up with both parties

  • Check with client after initial partner meeting
  • Check with partner on referral quality
  • Address any issues immediately

4. Monitor outcome

  • Track if referral converts to engagement
  • Gather feedback from both sides
  • Use learnings to improve future referrals

Goal: Make first 2-3 referrals exceptional quality to prove partnership value.

Partnership Maintenance

Monthly:

  • Informal check-in (email or quick call)
  • Share any client needs that might fit partner
  • Flag any client service issues immediately

Quarterly:

  • Formal partnership review meeting
  • Review referral volume and quality
  • Discuss what's working and what's not
  • Identify improvement opportunities
  • Plan any joint marketing initiatives

Annually:

  • Comprehensive partnership review
  • Renew or revise agreement terms
  • Set goals for coming year
  • Evaluate if partnership should expand or contract

Most partnerships fail due to neglect - schedule and honor these check-ins.

Partnership Marketing Opportunities

Once partnership is established:

Co-Marketing Ideas:

  • Joint webinar on complementary topics
  • Co-authored white paper or research
  • Case study featuring joint client success
  • Guest posts on each other's blogs
  • Social media cross-promotion
  • Joint attendance at industry events
  • Reciprocal newsletter features

Start small: One joint webinar > trying to do everything at once.

When to End a Partnership

End partnership if:

  • Referral quality is poor (reflecting badly on you)
  • Partner is unresponsive or unprofessional with your clients
  • Volume is too low to justify maintenance effort
  • Your business model changes and services no longer complement
  • Partner experiences reputational issues
  • Better partnership opportunities emerge (capacity is limited)

How to end gracefully:

  • Give notice per agreement (typically 30-90 days)
  • Complete any in-flight referrals professionally
  • Provide transition plan for shared clients
  • Be honest but diplomatic about reasons

Protect your reputation - end partnerships that aren't working rather than letting them damage your brand.

Scaling Partnership Networks

Start: 1-2 strategic partners

Focus: Proving model, building trust, refining process

Growth: 3-5 partners

Focus: Systemizing referrals, documenting standards

Scale: 5-10+ partners

Focus: Partner tiers (platinum, gold, silver), partner portal, automated tracking

Don't scale too fast: Better to have 3 excellent partners than 10 mediocre ones.

Partnership ROI Tracking

Metrics to monitor:

Volume metrics:

  • Referrals sent (count and value)
  • Referrals received (count and value)
  • Conversion rate (referrals → engagements)

Quality metrics:

  • Client satisfaction with referred work
  • Client retention rate for joint clients
  • Repeat referrals (indicates satisfaction)

Financial metrics:

  • Revenue from partnership referrals
  • Cost of partnership (time, fees paid, administration)
  • ROI vs. other client acquisition channels

Relationship metrics:

  • Response time to referrals
  • Communication quality
  • Issue resolution speed

Healthy partnership indicators:

  • Roughly balanced value exchange (within 70/30 over 12 months)
  • Client satisfaction equal to or better than direct clients
  • Increasing referral volume over time
  • Proactive communication from partner

Review quarterly - partnerships should improve business metrics, not just feel-good relationships.

Cold Email Personalization Framework

The 5-Minute Research Method for High-Converting Cold Emails

Why Personalization Matters

Industry data:

  • Personalized emails get 2-3x higher open rates
  • Personalized first lines increase response rates by 40-70%
  • Generic "spray and pray" emails get <1% response rate
  • Thoughtful, researched emails get 8-15% response rate

The rule: Spend 5 minutes researching each prospect. The ROI of this time investment is massive.

The 5-Minute Research Framework

Minute 1: LinkedIn Profile Scan (60 seconds)

What to look for:

  • Current role and how long they've been in it (new = different priorities than tenured)
  • Previous companies (industry experience, career trajectory)
  • Recent posts or activity (what they care about right now)
  • Shared connections (warm intro opportunities)
  • Education and location (cultural context, timezone)

Personalization opportunities:

  • "Saw you just joined [Company] as [Role]" (timing hook)
  • "Your post about [Topic] resonated" (engagement hook)
  • "We both know [Mutual Connection]" (warm intro hook)
  • "Noticed you worked at [Previous Company]" (shared experience hook)

Tools: LinkedIn (primary), LinkedIn Sales Navigator (advanced)

Minute 2: Company Website Scan (60 seconds)

What to look for:

  • What the company actually does (in their words, not your assumption)
  • Recent blog posts or news section (current priorities)
  • About page (company stage, team size, values)
  • Customer logos or case studies (who they serve, success metrics)
  • Job openings (hiring = growth = budget)

Personalization opportunities:

  • "Saw on your site that you recently [launched X, expanded to Y]"
  • "Noticed you work with [Industry] companies like [Customer Logo]"
  • "Your blog post about [Topic] made me think about [Problem You Solve]"
  • "Saw you're hiring for [Role] - usually means [Relevant Insight]"

Tools: Company website, blog, careers page

Minute 3: Recent News & Triggers (60 seconds)

What to look for:

  • Funding announcements (Crunchbase, press releases)
  • Product launches (Product Hunt, company blog, press)
  • Executive hires or departures (LinkedIn, press releases)
  • Expansion news (new office, new market, new product line)
  • Awards, recognition, or "Best of" lists

Personalization opportunities:

  • "Congrats on the Series [X] - typically when [Problem] becomes a priority"
  • "Saw you launched [Product] - curious how you're handling [Related Challenge]"
  • "Noticed you hired [Person] as [Role] - [Relevant Observation]"
  • "Read about your expansion into [Market] - we've helped other companies navigate [Challenge]"

Tools: Crunchbase, Google News search, LinkedIn company page, Product Hunt

Minute 4: Competitive/Industry Context (60 seconds)

What to look for:

  • Who their competitors are (G2, Capterra, BuiltWith for tech stack)
  • What tools/services they're likely using
  • Industry trends affecting them
  • Common challenges for their industry/stage
  • Regulatory or market shifts

Personalization opportunities:

  • "Noticed you're using [Tool] - we integrate with that"
  • "Given [Industry Trend], companies like yours are typically evaluating [Category]"
  • "With [Regulatory Change], [Their Company Type] companies need to [Action]"

  • Twitter/X activity (thought leadership, engagement style)

  • Reddit or community involvement (what they care about)

  • Custom video or audio message

  • Customized case study or resource

The Personalization Insertion Points

Every cold email template has 5 key insertion points for personalization:

1. Subject Line

Generic: "Quick question" Personalized: "[Company Name] - [Specific Observation]" Highly Personalized: "Your [LinkedIn post/recent launch/funding news] - quick thought"

2. Opening Line (First Sentence)

Generic: "I help companies with [X]" Personalized: "I noticed [Company] recently [specific trigger event]" Highly Personalized: "Your post about [specific topic] resonated - we see a lot of [their role] leaders facing [related challenge]"

This is the MOST important personalization point. Decision: continue reading or delete happens in the first 5 seconds.

3. Problem Statement

Generic: "Most companies struggle with [problem]" Personalized: "Most [their industry/stage] companies struggle with [specific problem]" Highly Personalized: "Based on [specific observation], I'd guess [Company] is dealing with [specific problem relevant to their situation]"

4. Social Proof

Generic: "We work with companies like [Random Logos]" Personalized: "We work with [their industry] companies like [Relevant Logo 1, 2]" Highly Personalized: "We recently helped [Similar Company in same situation] with [exact challenge you observed]. They achieved [specific outcome]."

5. Call to Action

Generic: "Want to chat?" Personalized: "Worth a 15-minute conversation about [specific topic relevant to them]?" Highly Personalized: "Worth 15 minutes to discuss how [Similar Company] handled [specific situation] when they were at your stage?"

Personalization Red Flags (What NOT to Do)

❌ Fake Personalization

Bad: "I was checking out your website and I'm impressed!" Why it's bad: Generic, could apply to anyone Better: "I was looking at your website - the case study about [Specific Client] was impressive, especially the [Specific Metric]"

❌ Creepy Over-Personalization

Bad: "I saw you vacationed in Hawaii based on your Instagram posts" Why it's bad: Too personal, feels stalkerish Better: "I saw you posted about [Professional Topic] on LinkedIn"

Rule: Stick to professional/public information. Don't reference personal life, family, non-work social media.

❌ Wrong Information

Bad: "Congrats on your Series B!" (when they raised Series A) Why it's bad: Shows you didn't actually research Better: Double-check facts before sending

❌ Assumptions Without Evidence

Bad: "I bet you're struggling with [X]" Why it's bad: You're guessing, might be totally wrong Better: "Most [their company type] companies at your stage deal with [X] - is that on your radar?"

❌ Overuse of Flattery

Bad: "Your company is amazing! You're doing incredible work!" Why it's bad: Sounds fake, brown-nosing Better: "Your approach to [Specific Thing] is interesting - we see most companies doing [Different Approach]"

Common Personalization Mistakes & Fixes

Mistake 1: "I love your company!"

Problem: Vague, meaningless flattery Fix: "Your approach to [Specific Thing] is interesting, especially [Specific Detail]"

Mistake 2: "I was impressed by your website"

Problem: Generic, sounds automated Fix: "I was looking at your website - the [Specific Page/Feature] section caught my attention because [Specific Reason]"

Mistake 3: "I think we could work together"

Problem: No evidence, just opinion Fix: "We've helped [Similar Companies] with [Specific Challenge] - based on [Specific Observation], thought it might be relevant to [Company Name]"

Mistake 4: Using Personalization Tokens Only

Problem: Just filling in [FIRST_NAME] and [COMPANY] fields Fix: Add at least one fully custom sentence based on your research

Mistake 5: Too Much Personalization

Problem: Email is all about them, no value proposition Fix: Personalized opening (1-2 sentences) → Value proposition → Personalized case study

Balance: 30% personalization, 70% value proposition and clear ask
