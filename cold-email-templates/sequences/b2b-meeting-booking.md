# B2B Services - Meeting Booking Sequence

**Industry:** B2B Professional Services (Consulting, Legal, Accounting, HR, etc.)
**Use Case:** Meeting Booking / Initial Consultation
**Sequence Length:** 5 emails over 30 days
**Best For:** Booking discovery calls with potential B2B service clients

---

## Email 1: Insight-Led Introduction (Day 0)

**Subject:** [Company Name] - quick observation about [specific area]

**Body:**

Hi [First Name],

I help [their company type] companies with [specific problem your service solves].

I was researching [their industry/company] and noticed [specific observation that indicates a problem or opportunity - e.g., "you recently expanded to 3 new states" or "your team grew from 20 to 50 people in the past year according to LinkedIn"].

In our experience, when [their company type] companies hit [milestone/growth stage], they typically face challenges with [specific problem - e.g., "multi-state compliance," "scaling HR processes," "financial reporting complexity"].

We've helped companies like [Similar Client 1] and [Similar Client 2] navigate [specific challenge] during similar growth phases.

Worth a 15-minute conversation to see if we can help [Company Name] avoid common pitfalls?

[Your Name]
[Your Company]

**Personalization Notes:**
- Reference specific company milestone (funding, expansion, hiring, product launch)
- Connect milestone to predictable challenge they'll face
- Name similar companies they'd recognize

**Subject:** Last note about [specific challenge]

**Personalization Notes:**
- Share insight from working with multiple clients (positions you as expert, not salesperson)
- Hint at future pain if not addressed (without being fear-mongering)
- Make it easy to ignore or engage

## B2B Services Cold Email Best Practices

### Industry-Specific Considerations:

**Legal Services:**
- Lead with risk mitigation, not cost savings
- Reference specific regulations or case law
- Emphasize confidentiality and credentials
- Longer sales cycle (3-6 months typical)

**Accounting/Finance:**
- Focus on tax savings and compliance
- Include certifications (CPA, etc.) in signature
- Send during tax planning season (Sept-Nov) for year-end work
- Be specific about specialization (startups vs. public vs. PE-backed)

**HR/People Ops:**
- Emphasize employee experience and compliance
- Reference company size transitions (20→50, 50→100 trigger points)
- Time outreach to funding rounds or expansion announcements
- Speak to founder/executive pain points (not just HR team)

**Consulting:**
- Lead with insights and frameworks, not services
- Position as strategic partner, not vendor
- Reference specific methodologies or IP
- Emphasize ROI and business outcomes

## Timing Strategies

### Best Times to Reach B2B Decision Makers:

**Monthly patterns:**
- **First 2 weeks:** Budget available, decision-making mode
- **Last 2 weeks:** Closing month-end work, less receptive
- **End of quarter:** Either extremely busy (bad time) or planning ahead (good time)

**Weekly patterns:**
- **Tuesday-Thursday:** Best response rates
- **Monday:** Catching up from weekend
- **Friday afternoon:** Winding down, less likely to book meetings

**Annual patterns:**
- **January-March:** New budget, high engagement
- **April:** Tax season (bad for accounting-related)
- **July-August:** Summer slowdown
- **September-October:** Planning for next year, good for consultative services
- **November-December:** Holiday slowdown, but good for "start in January" projects

## Expected Results

**B2B Services Email Benchmarks:**
- Response Rate: 7-12% (professional services have decent response rates)
- Meeting Booking Rate: 2-5%
- Meeting Show-Up Rate: 75-85% (higher than product sales)
- Meeting → Proposal: 50-70%
- Proposal → Client: 30-50%

**Overall Cold Email → New Client:** 0.5-1.5%

**Factors that improve results:**
- Narrow targeting (specific industry + company stage vs. broad outreach)
- Timing around trigger events (funding, expansion, regulatory changes)
- Demonstrable expertise (published content, speaking, credentials)
- Warm intro when possible (mutual clients, industry connections)

## Qualifying Questions for Discovery Calls

Once you book a meeting, qualify hard:

1. **"What prompted you to take this call?"**
   - Identifies urgency and motivation

2. **"What's your current approach to [problem area]?"**
   - Reveals if they're DIYing, using competitor, or have nothing in place

3. **"What's the cost of not solving this?"**
   - Quantifies pain (helps with pricing later)

4. **"What's your timeline for making a decision?"**
   - Qualifies urgency and budget availability

5. **"Who else needs to be involved in this decision?"**
   - Identifies if you're talking to decision-maker or influencer

6. **"What's your budget range for this?"**
   - Qualifies financial fit (ask tactfully but directly)

---

## Pricing Discussion Framework

For service-based businesses, pricing comes up early. Be prepared:

### Option 1: Range-Based
"Most [their company type] companies at your stage invest between $[X]-$[Y] depending on [scope factors]. Does that range work for your planning?"

### Option 2: Investment Framing
"This typically represents a $[X] investment with expected return of $[Y] through [specific outcomes]. Does that ROI make sense for [Company Name]?"

### Option 3: Tiered Options
"We have three approaches: [Basic] at $[X], [Standard] at $[Y], and [Premium] at $[Z]. Most clients in your situation start with [Standard]."

**Never say "it depends" without giving context.** Vague pricing kills deals.

---

## Red Flags (When to Walk Away)

**Don't pursue if:**
- They want free consulting disguised as "exploratory calls"
- Budget is dramatically below market rate (will be difficult client)
- Decision-maker keeps changing (internal dysfunction)
- They want results guarantees unrealistic for your service
- They've churned through multiple service providers recently (problem client pattern)
- They want to pay only on contingency (risk mismatch for most services)

**Your expertise is valuable** - qualify hard and walk away from bad fits.

## Proposal Best Practices

After discovery call, create proposal that includes:

1. **Executive Summary** - Their situation in 2-3 sentences
2. **Challenges Identified** - What you heard in discovery
3. **Proposed Approach** - Your methodology and timeline
4. **Deliverables** - Specific outputs they'll receive
5. **Investment** - Clear pricing (not "TBD")
6. **Team** - Who will work on this
7. **Case Studies** - 1-2 relevant examples
8. **Terms** - Payment schedule, timeline, cancellation
9. **Next Steps** - Clear call to action with timeline

**Keep it under 10 pages.** Decision-makers don't read 30-page proposals.

---

## Following Up After Proposal

**Day 0 (send day):** "Proposal sent - available for questions"
**Day 3:** "Quick check-in - any questions on the proposal?"
**Day 7:** "Following up - what questions can I answer?"
**Day 10:** "Final check-in - should I close this file or adjust the proposal?"

**After 2 weeks with no response:** Assume it's a no. Move on.

**Exception:** If they say "circling back in [timeframe]," honor that and follow up when they said.
